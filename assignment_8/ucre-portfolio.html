Bias in Voice Assistants

I completed a research project examining how users dealt with biases and errors in voice assistants.

Context/Problem

In my user research class, my team and I gathered insights about how users deal with biases and errors in voice assistants and we came up with a rough design solution.

Process

Research

My team, consisting of me and Nali Huynh, Sohail Bagheri, Mahit Munakala, started by defining a rough problem statement based on some light background research and reading. We wanted to make it easier for users to report biases and errors in voice assistants.

We started by conducting think-alouds with multiple recruited users to uncover insights about their experiences with their voice assistants. After gathering some rough notes we consolidated their interviews into affinity diagrams to see if there were any commonalities. We then ran directed story-telling sessions with users to walk through their voice-assistant history. From this, and subsequent affinity diagramming, we came up with 3 insights: 1. Users think it's their fault when voice assistants mess up. 2. Users don't know if or how they can report issues. 3. Users want their feedback to make a difference.

Design

We then validated these needs and tested out new design ideas using the speed-dating method, where you present multiple storyboards to a user, each highlighting a different potential design solution.

Solution

Finally, we arrived at our design solution: When the voice assistant detects the user's frustration in their syntax, it initiates a simple error report process by sending a push notification to their phone. Then, in future updates, specific user contributions and feedback will be called out, to make the user feel like their feedback actually mattered.
